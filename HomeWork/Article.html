THE VOICES IN OUR HEADS

Why do people talk to themselves, and when does it become a problem?
By Jerome Groopman
"T alking to your yogurt again," my wife, Pam, said. "And what does the yogurt say?"
She had caught me silently talking to myself as we ate breakfast. A conversation was playing in my mind, with a research colleague who questioned whether we had sufficient data to go ahead and publish. Did the experiments in the second graph need to be repeated? The results wre already solid, I answered. But then, on reflection, I agreed that repetion could make the statistics more compelling.
